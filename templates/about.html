THE MONTY HALL PROBLEM

The Monty Hall problem, named after the host of the American television game show "Let's Make a Deal," has captivated mathematicians, statisticians, and enthusiasts since it emerged. Despite its apparent simplicity, this probability puzzle defies intuition and challenges our understanding of chances.

The Game

Here’s how it works:

There are 3 doors, two of them with a goat behind it, one with a car.

The contestant picks whatever door they think might have the car

The host opens a door with a goat, thus eliminating one losing door

The contestant is given the option to switch to the other unopened door or to stay with the chosen door

At this point, most of us would probably just go with out gut feeling or even be inclined to stay with our initial choice. That's not be a good strategy, however, if you want the odds to stay in your favour.

A Question from a Reader

In 1990, a reader submitted the following question to the magazine "Parade" asking their inhouse genius, Marilyn Von Savant:

So, in short, does it make a difference if you switch doors on "Let's Make a Deal"?

A Controversial Answer

Marilyn Von Savant had a somewhat surprising answer to this question:

Switch and double your chances, she told the reader. Her answer doesn't seem to make sense. With 1 car and 1 goat left surely it doesn't matter if you switch doors? Logic tells us that each door had a 1/3 chance of holding the car when the game started. After the host eliminated a goat door, the odds must be 1/2 for each remaining door. Either your chosen door has the car or the other door has it, a 50/50 case? Right?

The Community Responds

Not according to Mary Von Savant, it seemed. Using her perplexing example, she claimed that your winning chances double if you switch doors. It makes no sense.

This seemed to general the consensus and Marilyn’s answer generated a lot of heat

(I presented my cousin, who is smart, has a master's degree and works with statistics, with this puzzle. He absolutely rejected it and I ended up letting it go)

Marilyn persisted, however. And, as the months went on, simulations were run and numbers double checked and some very different responses began to emerge.

The Final Solution

As it turned out, the response to the readers question in Parade was completely correct- you should definitely switch doors if given the option.

This bit of statistics is a counter intuitive as they get. The fact remains, however, that the chosen door intially had a 1/3 chance of having a car behind it and those odds stay the same throughout the game. The solution can even be calculated using something called "Bayes' theorem".
